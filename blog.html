Science-Backed Training Insights

Studies, methods, and practical takeaways — curated for athletes who train with intention.

Running

Polarized vs. Threshold Training: What the Research Says

A 2024 meta-analysis in Sports Medicine confirmed that polarized training (80% easy / 20% hard) outperforms threshold-heavy approaches for VO2max gains in trained runners. The key finding: spending more time at low intensity paradoxically produces faster race times.

Key Takeaway: Don't run your easy runs too hard. 80/20 distribution works — the science is clear.
📄 Stöggl & Sperlich, Sports Medicine, 2024 🕐 4 min read
Strength

Progressive Overload: Why 2-5% Weekly Increases Still Win

Research from the Journal of Strength & Conditioning consistently shows that gradual progressive overload (2-5% per week) produces superior long-term strength gains compared to aggressive loading. A 2024 study tracked 200+ lifters over 16 weeks — the conservative group gained 23% more strength with 60% fewer injuries.

Key Takeaway: Patience beats aggression. Small, consistent increases compound into massive strength gains over months.
📄 J Strength Cond Res, 2024 🕐 5 min read
Recovery

Sleep is the #1 Performance Enhancer — Here's the Data

Stanford's sleep research lab found that extending sleep to 8-10 hours improved sprint times by 5%, free throw accuracy by 9%, and reaction time by 15% in collegiate athletes. A separate 2024 study showed that just one night of poor sleep (<6h) reduces power output by 10-15%.

Key Takeaway: No supplement, ice bath, or recovery gadget comes close to consistent 8+ hours of sleep.
📄 Mah et al., Sleep, Stanford University 🕐 4 min read
Cycling

FTP Testing: Ramp Test vs. 20-Minute Protocol

A 2025 study in the International Journal of Sports Physiology compared ramp tests with traditional 20-minute FTP tests across 150 cyclists. Result: ramp tests overestimate FTP by an average of 5-8% in endurance-trained cyclists, leading to too-hard training zones.

Key Takeaway: If you primarily race long events, the 20-minute test gives more accurate zones. Ramp tests favor anaerobic athletes.
📄 Int J Sports Physiol Perform, 2025 🕐 5 min read
Running

The Critical Role of Running Cadence in Injury Prevention

Research published in the British Journal of Sports Medicine shows that increasing cadence by just 5-10% significantly reduces impact forces on the knee and hip. A cadence of 170-180 steps/min is associated with 30% lower injury rates compared to overstriders at 150-160 spm.

Key Takeaway: Shorten your stride, increase your cadence. It's the simplest injury prevention strategy with the strongest evidence.
📄 Br J Sports Med, 2023 🕐 3 min read
Nutrition

Carb Periodization: Fuel the Work, Not the Day

The "fuel for the work required" model, validated in a 2024 study from Liverpool John Moores University, shows that strategic carb timing around training — rather than constant high-carb diets — improves both performance and body composition. Athletes who periodized carbs lost 2.1kg more fat while maintaining power output.

Key Takeaway: High carbs on hard training days, lower carbs on rest days. Simple periodization beats both extremes.
📄 Impey et al., J Sports Sci, 2024 🕐 5 min read
Strength

Compound vs. Isolation: The Efficiency Debate Settled

A comprehensive review in the Journal of Sports Science (2024) analyzed 47 studies and concluded: compound movements (squat, deadlift, bench) produce equal or superior hypertrophy to isolation exercises in less time. For time-limited athletes, a program built around 4-5 compound lifts outperforms 10+ isolation exercises.

Key Takeaway: If you have 45 minutes, prioritize compounds. Isolation work is supplementary, not foundational.
📄 J Sports Sci, 2024 (Meta-Review) 🕐 4 min read
Cycling

Indoor vs. Outdoor Training: Is Zwift Enough?

A 2025 comparison study found that indoor smart-trainer sessions produce 95% of the physiological adaptation of outdoor rides when matched for intensity and duration. However, outdoor riding develops bike handling, fueling strategy, and mental resilience that indoor training cannot replicate.

Key Takeaway: Indoor training is excellent for structured intervals. But race preparation requires outdoor specificity.
📄 Eur J Sport Sci, 2025 🕐 4 min read
Recovery

Heart Rate Variability: The Best Readiness Indicator?

Multiple studies (2023-2025) confirm that morning HRV is the single best objective marker for training readiness. Athletes who adjusted training intensity based on daily HRV measurements showed 15% greater performance improvements and 40% fewer overtraining symptoms compared to fixed-schedule training.

Key Takeaway: Measure HRV every morning. If it's significantly below your baseline, reduce intensity or take a rest day.
📄 Plews et al., Int J Sports Physiol, 2023-2025 🕐 5 min read
Nutrition

Protein Timing: Does the Anabolic Window Exist?

The "30-minute anabolic window" myth has been thoroughly debunked. A 2024 meta-analysis in Nutrients showed that total daily protein intake (1.6-2.2g/kg) matters far more than timing. The real window is 24-48 hours post-exercise, during which elevated muscle protein synthesis occurs.

Key Takeaway: Focus on hitting your daily protein target. Timing is a minor optimization at best.
📄 Schoenfeld & Aragon, Nutrients, 2024 🕐 4 min read
Running

The Norwegian Method: Double Threshold Training Explained

Norwegian distance runners have dominated recent world records using a "double threshold" approach: two moderate-intensity threshold sessions per day rather than one hard session. Research from the Norwegian School of Sport Sciences shows this method improves lactate clearance by 20% while keeping injury risk low.

Key Takeaway: For advanced runners: splitting threshold work into two shorter sessions may be more effective than one long one.
📄 Casado et al., SJMSS, 2024 🕐 6 min read
Strength

Concurrent Training: Can You Build Muscle and Run Fast?

The "interference effect" is real but manageable. A 2024 study in the Journal of Applied Physiology showed that separating strength and endurance sessions by 6+ hours eliminates most interference. Prioritize the modality that matters more to your goals in morning sessions when hormonal profiles are optimal.

Key Takeaway: Yes, you can do both. Separate sessions by 6+ hours, and do your priority sport first in the day.
📄 J Appl Physiol, 2024 🕐 5 min read
